How to Keep Kids Entertained at a Restaurant (While You Wait for Food)

20 no-prep games and activities — most of them screen-free — to keep toddlers, preschoolers, and big kids happy from the moment you sit down until the food arrives.

You've ordered, the drinks have arrived, and the crayons that came with the kids' menu lasted about ninety seconds. The food is still fifteen minutes out, and you can feel the restlessness building across the table. Every parent knows this stretch — the wait between ordering and eating is where restaurant meltdowns are born.

The good news: you don't need a bag of gadgets to get through it. A few no-prep games — most of which use nothing but your words or what's already on the table — can turn that dreaded wait into some of the most fun you'll have all meal. And the best ones aren't about distracting your kids so you can check out — they're about doing something together, so the wait becomes connection time instead of screen time.

Below are 20 things to do with kids while you wait for food, grouped by type and age, plus a few tips to set your table up for success before the fussing starts. They work just as well in a waiting room, at the DMV, or on a long car ride.

The Short Answer: How to Keep Kids Busy at a Restaurant

The best way to keep kids entertained at a restaurant is to have a handful of no-prep games ready and rotate through them. Talking games like I Spy and 20 Questions, quick paper games like tic-tac-toe, and building with things already on the table need zero equipment and settle kids in seconds. Order their food the moment you sit down, and save one calm screen option — like a shared trivia game — for if the wait runs long.

When the restlessness starts, reach for one of these five categories:

  • No-equipment talking games — I Spy, 20 Questions, Would You Rather
  • Games using what's on the table — stacking packets, straw wrappers, napkin fun
  • Paper-and-crayon games on the kids' menu — tic-tac-toe, hangman, squiggle drawing
  • Quiet toddler wins — peekaboo, sticker pads, color hunts
  • One smart screen option — a shared trivia game, not a solo video

Why Waiting for Food Is So Hard for Little Kids

A restaurant asks a lot of a small child all at once: sit still, stay quiet, use nice manners, and wait patiently for something you can smell but can't eat yet. The thrill of arriving wears off in minutes, and there's nothing to do with all that energy. Understanding why the wait is so hard makes it easier to get ahead of a meltdown instead of just reacting to one. Here's what's really going on.

🍽️ Hunger Meets Boredom

Kids arrive hungry and then have to wait right next to the smell of food with nothing to do — a tough combination for a small nervous system.

🪑 Sitting Still Is a Big Ask

Young children aren't built to sit quietly for twenty minutes. Their bodies need to move, and a chair at a table works against that instinct.

⏳ Novelty Wears Off Fast

The crayons and the new place are exciting for about three minutes. After that, the clock starts ticking toward restlessness.

📏 Lots of Rules at Once

Inside voice, stay seated, don't touch that — restaurants pile on expectations that are genuinely hard for a young child to hold all at the same time.

20 Things to Do With Kids While You Wait for Food

Here are 20 ideas grouped into five easy categories. Mix and match a couple from different groups, and rotate to a new one the moment interest starts to fade — variety is what carries you to the finish line.

💛 One thing before the list: the goal isn't just to keep kids quiet — it's to be with them. Notice that nearly every idea here is something you do together. A few unhurried minutes of your full attention are worth more than any gadget, and the wait is a surprisingly good chance to talk, laugh, and hear what's on their mind. Reach for a screen only when you've genuinely run out of steam — and even then, play it together.

Games Using What’s Already on the Table

Sugar-Packet Tower — Stack sugar and creamer packets into the tallest tower you can before it topples — surprisingly gripping for small hands.

Best for ages 3+

Tic-Tac-Toe — The fastest game on paper. Best of five keeps it going, and little kids love finally beating a grown-up.

Set Your Table Up for Success

The games do a lot of the work, but a little planning before you even walk in makes the whole meal smoother. These small moves head off the most common wait-time meltdowns.

1. Order Kid Food the Moment You Sit

Ask for their meal — plus a quick starter like bread or fruit — as soon as you order drinks. A small snack in the first few minutes buys you the crucial early window.

2. Ask for the Little Extras

A cup with a lid, extra napkins, or a side of fruit can head off spills and hunger tantrums before they start. Most servers are happy to help when you ask early.

3. Pick Your Seat and Timing Wisely

A booth contains wigglers better than a chair, a corner keeps toddlers away from walkways, and an earlier dinner beats the overtired rush-hour meltdown.

4. Pack a Tiny Restaurant Kit

Keep a small pouch with crayons, a couple of quiet toys, a sticker pad, and wipes. Because it only comes out at restaurants, it stays interesting week after week.

5. Set Two Simple Expectations

Before you walk in, agree on a couple of easy rules — "bottoms stay on seats, inside voices" — so kids know the plan instead of learning it in the moment.

6. Tag-Team a Lap When Needed

If the wait runs long, take turns walking a restless little one around the lobby or over to look at the fish tank. A quick change of scenery resets everyone.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I keep my toddler entertained at a restaurant?

No-prep games work best for toddlers: stack sugar packets, play peekaboo with a napkin, go on a color hunt, or hand over a reusable sticker pad. Keep each activity short and rotate often, since a toddler’s attention span is only a few minutes.

What can kids do at a restaurant without screens?

Kids can play I Spy, 20 Questions, or Would You Rather, draw tic-tac-toe and hangman on the kids’ menu, build with things already on the table, or go on a color-and-shape hunt. Most need zero equipment and settle kids within seconds.

How do you keep kids busy while waiting for food?

Order their meal (plus a quick starter like bread or fruit) the moment you sit down, then rotate through two or three short games so nothing gets stale before the food arrives. Having a few ideas ready beats scrambling once the fussing starts.

What should I pack in a restaurant kit for kids?

A few crayons and paper, a couple of small quiet toys or finger puppets, a reusable sticker pad, and wipes. Keep it tiny so it fits in your bag, and only bring it out at restaurants so it stays interesting.

Is it okay to let kids use a screen at a restaurant?

An occasional screen is fine, but the best entertainment at the table is still you — talking, playing, and paying attention. When you do reach for a device, choose an interactive, shared option over passive video: a single-device game like Family Trivia keeps the whole table involved instead of pulling one child away.

The Wait Doesn't Have to Be the Hard Part

Eating out with kids doesn't have to mean bracing for the stretch between ordering and eating. With a few games in your back pocket — and a snack ordered the second you sit down — you can keep the whole table happy from the first "I'm bored" to the last bite. Most of these ideas cost nothing, fit in your memory instead of your bag, and work just as well in a waiting room or on a long drive.

And here's the quiet upside: those fifteen minutes of waiting can be some of the best face-to-face time in your day. Played together, a round of I Spy or a silly story chain turns "time to kill" into real connection — the kind kids remember long after they've forgotten what they ordered. The screen is there if you need it, but you're the best thing at the table.

Pick two or three favorites to try next time you go out. And the games your kids love at the restaurant have a way of coming home with you — when they do, turn them into a weekly tradition with our family game night ideas.
